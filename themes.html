Conference Themes

Six interconnected themes guiding our dialogue about human futures in the age of intelligent systems

Theme 1

Epistemic Pluralism: What Knowledges Matter?

This theme examines how intelligent systems shape the production, validation, and circulation of knowledge—and asks what forms of knowledge are being marginalized, transformed, or lost. It explores normative questions about epistemic justice and cognitive diversity in an age of algorithmic mediation.

Subtopics:

  • Epistemic justice and epistemic injustice in AI-mediated knowledge systems
  • Coloniality of knowledge and the politics of recognition
  • Cognitive justice and the valuation of diverse ways of knowing
  • Indigenous and local epistemologies in relation to global knowledge infrastructures
  • Multilingual knowledge production and the fate of non-dominant languages
  • Language, power, and the politics of translation
  • Knowledge sovereignty and data governance
  • Global inequalities in scientific knowledge production
  • Decolonizing research methodologies
  • Counter-hegemonic knowledge practices
Theme 2

Reimagining Education: What Capacities Should We Cultivate?

This theme asks what education should become when intelligent systems perform many tasks traditionally associated with learning and cognition. It explores pedagogical, curricular, and institutional transformations needed to cultivate human capacities that algorithms cannot replace—and that societies should value.

Subtopics:

  • Aims of education in the age of AI
  • Educational equity and the digital divide
  • Higher education transformation and institutional futures
  • Curriculum futures: what should be taught and why?
  • Critical pedagogy and emancipatory education
  • Postdigital education and hybrid learning environments
  • Learner agency and autonomy in algorithmically structured settings
  • Critical thinking, creativity, and metacognition
  • Educational leadership for just and human-centered futures
  • Lifelong learning and the changing nature of work
  • Assessment and recognition of learning in new contexts
  • Teacher and educator roles in intelligent learning environments
Theme 3

Intelligent Systems and Knowledge Ecologies: How Should We Design?

This theme examines the design, governance, and ethical dimensions of intelligent systems as they reshape knowledge creation, dissemination, and use. It asks what principles and practices should guide the development of technologies that are truly human-centered.

Subtopics:

  • Generative AI and the transformation of knowledge production
  • Algorithmic governance and its implications for knowledge
  • Platformization of education and knowledge systems
  • Digital sovereignty and public alternatives to private platforms
  • AI ethics and responsible innovation
  • Explainable AI and algorithmic transparency
  • Human-AI collaboration and co-intelligence
  • Algorithmic bias, fairness, and accountability
  • Digital inequality and access to knowledge infrastructures
  • Open knowledge, commons, and public goods
  • Participatory design and democratic governance of intelligent systems
  • Environmental sustainability of knowledge technologies
Theme 4

Language, Culture, and Meaning: What Futures for Cultural Vitality?

This theme explores how intelligent technologies interact with language, culture, and meaning—shaping what is preserved, translated, amplified, or erased. It asks how cultural and linguistic diversity can be sustained and enriched in an age of global digital infrastructures.

Subtopics:

  • Discourse and ideology in digital publics
  • Digital discourse and changing communicative practices
  • Corpus linguistics and the analysis of AI-mediated text
  • Language policy in multilingual digital environments
  • Pragmatics and the changing nature of meaning-making
  • Sociolinguistics of digital interaction
  • Literary futures: reading, writing, and authorship in AI contexts
  • Translation technologies and cultural mediation
  • Multilingualism, language preservation, and revitalization
  • Cultural narratives and counter-narratives
  • Cultural memory, heritage, and digital preservation
  • Algorithmic curation of cultural content
Theme 5

Humanities in Transition: What Place for Critical, Creative, and Interpretive Traditions?

This theme examines the transformation of humanities disciplines, practices, and values as intelligent systems increasingly participate in cultural and interpretive work. It asks how humanities traditions can inform the design of intelligent systems and how they themselves must evolve.

Subtopics:

  • Future of literature and literary study
  • Reading cultures in the digital age
  • Digital humanities: methods, tools, and critiques
  • Cultural memory and the politics of forgetting
  • Arts and AI: creation, curation, and reception
  • Philosophy of technology and the human condition
  • Heritage preservation and cultural continuity
  • Media ecology and knowledge environments
  • Human creativity in relation to machine intelligence
  • Critical AI literacy and its cultivation
  • The public humanities and civic engagement
  • Humanities perspectives on algorithmic culture
Theme 6

Human Flourishing and Democratic Life: What Societies Should We Build?

This theme asks broad normative questions about the kind of societies we should cultivate as intelligent systems become pervasive. It explores relationships among technology, well-being, justice, participation, and sustainability.

Subtopics:

  • Well-being, happiness, and the good life in knowledge societies
  • Resilience and human capacities in contexts of rapid change
  • Emotional intelligence and affective dimensions of human-AI interaction
  • Ethics of technology design, deployment, and governance
  • Democratic citizenship and participation in algorithmic societies
  • Sustainability and the environmental costs of knowledge technologies
  • Gender justice and intersectional equity
  • Inclusive societies and the politics of recognition
  • Future competencies and capabilities for democratic life
  • Social cohesion and community in digitally mediated contexts
  • Justice, rights, and governance of knowledge systems
  • Utopian and dystopian imaginaries of technological futures
