About the Conference

Background

Intelligent technologies are no longer peripheral tools but increasingly integral to how knowledge is produced, validated, and shared; how education is delivered and experienced; how cultures are preserved, translated, and transformed; and how societies organize themselves.

This integration is not merely technical but deeply normative: it embodies choices about what counts as knowledge, whose voices matter, what capacities are worth cultivating, and what futures are worth pursuing.

The Central Question

"What kinds of knowledge, educational systems, cultures, and societies do we want to cultivate as intelligent technologies become integral to human life?"

This question moves beyond assessing impacts or predicting outcomes toward intentional design and ethical imagination. It asks us to articulate aspirations, examine trade-offs, and consider how intelligent systems might be shaped to support—rather than undermine—human flourishing, epistemic pluralism, educational equity, cultural vitality, and democratic life.

The Conference Invitation

The conference invites scholars, educators, technologists, and practitioners to engage in critical and constructive dialogue about the futures we are co-creating and to surface the values, assumptions, and power dynamics embedded in emerging knowledge ecologies. It asks not only what is happening, but what should happen—and how we might get there.

Guiding Questions

  • What forms of knowledge are most worth cultivating, and how should intelligent systems be designed to support rather than distort them?
  • What educational aims, pedagogies, and institutional forms are needed to prepare learners for lives of meaning, agency, and critical engagement in a technologically saturated world?
  • Which cultural and linguistic heritages must be actively preserved and amplified, and how can technologies serve cultural vitality rather than cultural homogenization?
  • What societal values—justice, pluralism, democracy, sustainability, care—must guide the design and governance of intelligent systems?
  • How can we ensure that intelligent technologies expand epistemic diversity rather than consolidate dominant knowledge systems?
  • What new literacies, competencies, and dispositions are required for meaningful participation in knowledge societies?
  • Who should decide, and through what processes, what futures are desirable and how to pursue them?
  • How might we resist reductionist visions of intelligence, education, and human value in favor of richer, more capacious accounts?

Our Approach

Beyond Critique

Moving past critique and prediction toward imagination, deliberation, and design of the futures we are co-creating.

Interdisciplinary

Bringing together diverse perspectives from humanities, sciences, technology, education, and practice communities.

Normative

Asking not only what is happening, but what should happen—and examining the values embedded in technological systems.

Participatory

Centering the voices, experiences, and knowledge of those most affected by intelligent systems and their governance.
